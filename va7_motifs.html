Valsgärde 7

Motifs of Vendel Period Helmet Plates and Related Objects

The enumaration of motifs follows an upcoming MA Thesis and includes more than only the motifs of the Va. 7 helmet.

Most motifs are not yet properply described, but will be upon submission of the upcoming thesis.

M1: Man between the Bears

Reconstruction of the motif on Va7 by Arwidsson (1977)

This motif is present on the Va. 7 helmet as two fragments of the fourth plate row positioned frontal on the left and right side.

In the North-Germanic context this motif is known from the Torslunda Patrice A.
Similar motifs are found on the Staffordshire Purse and Pressbleckdisks of Eschwege-Niederhorne Grave 7.

The motif's connection to the Daniel-Motif remains unclear, but it has been suggested as the origin with re-interpration in the Germanic context as a scene from a heroic lay (cf. Böhner 1991).


A few examples of related scenes from literature (see Beck 1968, 241ff. for more details) are as follows:

I. Scenes of "real" hunt ("Das Überwinden von jagdbaren Tieren wird als Indiz für Heldenhaftigkeit betrachtet" Beck 1968).

1. Niebelungenlied Lied 16: Siegfried at the bear-hunt.

2. Gesta Danorum 1.11.: Scioldus binds a bear.

3. Grettis saga Ásmundarsonar (Ch. 21): Grettir achieves great fame by overcoming a bear that eight other men were unable to defeat.

4. Several examples of hunting being a test of prowess in the Fornaldarsögur, e.g. Örvar-Oddr.

II. Scenes of "fictive" hunt (as a cipher for struggle with human opponents)

1. Niebelungenlied (cf. above): Siegfried is killed right after the hunting scene. After Siegfried's death, the real hunt becomes a parable in which the hero is both the hunter and the hunted animal.

2. Helgakviða Hundingsbana ǫnnur 8: parable for the battle with King Hunding
„er ek bjǫrnu tók í Bragalundi / ok ætt ara oddum saddak.“
„when I captured bears in Bragalundr / and sated the family of eagles with spear-points.” (Pettit 2023, p. 438-439)

3. Fornmanna (/Konunga) sögur, Jómsvíkíngasaga: a bóndi challenges Jómsvikings to a bear hunt = fight against jarl Hákon. („væri yðr meiri veiðr at taka björninn, er nú er kominn á bjarn básinn.“)

4. Atlakviða in gr. 11: "Black fanged bears will bite with their teeth" (birnir blacfiallir / bíta preftǫnnom), i.e. the endeavour will not be easy, says Gunnar before the journey to the hostile Hun country.

M2: Binding of the Beast

Torslunda B

This motif is not present on the Va. 7 helmet, but on Torslunda patrice B, Vendel I helmet and fragments of Vendel XI helmet.

Most likely related to the motif M1 - not only because it has been found with Torslunda patrice A, but also because of stylistc similarity.

Against "bear-like" or unspecified, but not wyrm-like, beasts the following fight: Beowulf (vs. Grendel), Örvar Oddr, Herrauðr, Hrómundr, Ormr Stórólfsson, and certainly a few more.

Different interpretations have been put forward: Thor carrying one of his goats (Olrik 1918), Ragnar loðbrók ("Ragnar hairy-breeches") with his protective fur-pants (Schück 1926), re-interpretation of a heroic dragon fight (Hauck 1975), bear binding as a cult act (Höfler 1961), similarly, depiction of a rite-of-passage ritual for young "odinic" warriors (Nordberg 2003), a berserkr (various, e.g. Beck 1964), and Týr and Wolf Fenrir (e.g. Oehrl 2010).

M3-2: Warrior Procession with Eagle-Helmets

Reconstruction of the motif on Va7 by Arwidsson (1977)

This motif is present on the first plate row on the right part of the Va. 7 helmet.

The birds of preys, or perhaps eagles, depicted on the helmets, have been often put in context of war and Odin (Beck 1964, p. 25-31, Oehrl 2020 455ff.):

1. Odin = Arnhǫfði = "the eagle-headed"

2. Odin is ability to transform into an eagle, as attested in the story of Skaldenmet in Skáldskaparmál):
“þá brásk hann í arnarham ok flaug sem ákafasti.”
“Then he turned himself into the form of an eagle and flew as hard as he could.” (Faulkes 1995, p. 64 & 1998, p.4)

3. Kennings such as ǫrno seðia = feeding the eagle, hrafna seðia = feed the ravens, bergir hræsævar = taster of the corpse-sea = raven, and >Óðins haukr = raven.

M4: The Duelers

Ve12

This motif is not present in Va. 7, but on Vendel 12, 14 and 11 helmets.

This motif is generally so wide-spread, that it is hard to connect to one specific story.

An example is Hildebrandslied with a father vs. son duel.
